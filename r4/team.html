Our Team

image detail from book titled etc.

This digital exhibit – and the overarching Eileen Southern Initiative (ESI) that encompasses it – was developed at Harvard University in 2019-21, beginning just before the disruptions of COVID-19. Please visit our ABOUT PAGE for more about the genesis of the project. Initially, the ESI was conceived as a library-classroom collaboration, which remained the case, even as the scope expanded substantially. Students in an American Studies seminar conducted initial interviews with Southern’s colleagues, as well as her academic progeny, and they undertook preliminary curation of the exhibit. Since then, a dedicated cluster of Harvard students, faculty, and staff has been steadily involved. We are grateful to them all.

First and foremost, we extend thanks to Katie Callam (Postdoctoral Fellow, Harvard GSAS Fellowships & Writing Center), who was a central force in completing the digital exhibit. Her title was Project Manager, but her position quickly evolved into a fusion of writer, researcher, editor, and IT guru. She was a wizard at working with Omeka, the open-source web platform that provided the exhibit’s IT infrastructure, and she dedicated herself to pursuing long-buried documentation about Southern’s racial and gender struggles.

We want to single out two members of our core leadership team

Braxton D. Shelley

(formerly of Harvard and currently Associate Professor of Music, of Sacred Music, and of Divinity, Yale University), a steady partner who generously contributed innovative ideas.

Andrew Clark

(Director of Choral Activities and Senior Lecturer in Music), who brought this project to Harvard undergraduates, both in the classroom and through the performances of Harvard’s choirs. Andy spearheaded our collaboration with the Aeolians from Oakwood University, an HBCU in Alabama.

The Division of Arts & Humanities at Harvard

which through Dean Robin Kelsey provided two separate grants in support of the ESI

Judith B. Walzer

Emerita Provost of The New School for Social Research in New York City

whose extensive 1981 interview with Southern served as a key source for the digital exhibit. At the time of the interview, Walzer was Director of the Office of Women’s Education at Radcliffe College and later Assistant Dean for Coeducation at Harvard College. We are grateful for her initiative and foresight in interviewing the women – only 13 of them – who were then tenured in Harvard’s Faculty of Arts and Sciences.
